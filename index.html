2026 Florida Alimony Estimator: Calculate Spousal Support Caps & Guidelines

Last month, I sat down with a friend who was terrified about her upcoming divorce. She had no idea how she was going to pay her bills after twenty years of marriage. Going through a divorce creates so much financial uncertainty and stress. I get it. We've all been there. That is exactly why I built this Florida alimony estimator.

A lot of people heard about the massive 2023 alimony reform but don't know what it means. The lawmakers passed Senate Bill 1416, which completely overhauled Florida Statute § 61.08. The biggest shock for most families? Permanent alimony is officially dead for new cases. It does not exist anymore. This estimator uses the brand new durational alimony caps to show you exactly what to expect.

Types of Alimony in Florida

Since permanent support is gone, judges use five specific categories to handle financial transitions.

Type What It Does Limits
Temporary (Pendente Lite) Pays the bills while the divorce is still active in court. Ends the day the judge signs the final divorce decree.
Bridge-the-Gap Covers short-term needs like moving expenses or an apartment deposit so you can transition to single life. Hard capped at two years. You can't modify this later.
Rehabilitative Funds specific education or job training so you can support yourself. Capped at five years. You must submit a detailed plan to the court.
Durational Provides ongoing monthly support based on your income difference. This is the new standard replacement for permanent alimony. Length depends strictly on how many years you were married.
Lump-Sum Buyouts Trades monthly payments for a single upfront cash transfer or a larger share of house equity. Requires both parties to negotiate and agree on the present-value math.

How is Alimony Calculated in Florida?

Everything starts with two basic requirements. The spouse asking for money must prove they actually have a financial need. The other spouse must have the actual ability to pay it. If both exist, the math begins.

The courts look at gross income first. That means everything you earn from salaries, quarterly bonuses, business income, investment dividends, and retirement accounts. But judges don't base the final award on gross income. They calculate your statutory net income. They take your gross pay and strip out income taxes, mandatory union dues, and your health insurance premiums.

The 35% Rule Calculation Example

Florida strictly caps durational alimony at 35 percent of the difference between those two net incomes. Say you take home $10,000 a month and your ex takes home $4,000. The difference is $6,000. The absolute highest amount a judge can order is $2,100 a month.

Business owners complicate this calculation. If you run a company and expense your personal car lease and cell phone through the business, the judge will add that money back into your income.

Child support also changes the math. Courts always calculate child support first. Paying child support lowers your available net income, which directly shrinks the maximum alimony you can be forced to pay.

How Long Does Alimony Last? (The Duration Cliffs)

The law defines exactly how long you have to pay based on the length of your marriage.

  • Short-Term Marriages (Under 10 years): The court presumes you don't need alimony. If you somehow prove you do, the payments cannot last longer than 50 percent of the length of the marriage.
  • Moderate-Term Marriages (10 to 20 years): You can receive support for a maximum of 60 percent of the marriage duration. If you stayed married for 15 years, the payments hit a hard stop at 9 years.
  • Long-Term Marriages (20+ years): The court presumes you probably need support. Even then, the payments are capped at 75 percent of the marriage length.

Judges only ignore these duration caps under exceptional circumstances. You have to prove a severe, permanent disability or show that you are stuck at home full-time caring for a severely disabled child.

Key Legal Factors Judges Must Consider (Florida Statute 61.08)

Judges review a specific list of factors before they finalize the numbers. They look at the standard of living established during the marriage, but they also know two households cost more to run than one. They evaluate both spouses' resources, existing assets, and education levels to figure out earning capacity.

Your unpaid contributions matter. A judge assigns value to homemaking, raising kids, and sacrificing your own career to support your partner. They also review the mental and physical health of both people to see if a medical condition ruins someone's ability to work.

Adultery still plays a role in a no-fault state. If an affair drained marital funds on hotel rooms and expensive dinners, the judge will heavily weigh that financial waste when deciding the alimony amount.

A valid prenuptial or postnuptial agreement overrides all of this. If you signed a legal contract waving your right to spousal support, the statutory calculator means nothing. The contract wins.

Modifications, Terminations & Special Circumstances

Things change after the divorce. A payor reaching normal retirement age can file a petition to reduce or completely terminate their monthly support obligation. The court also cuts off payments if the recipient enters a supportive relationship. Cohabitation terminates alimony, but you have to prove it in court. You need hard evidence like shared bank accounts, a joint lease, or proof they split the grocery bills at a shared residence.

Some people just quit their jobs to avoid paying. That doesn't work. If a judge decides you are voluntarily underemployed, they will impute your income. They calculate your alimony based on what you should be earning, and you still have to pay it.

Courts sometimes force the paying spouse to secure the alimony with a life insurance policy. This only happens in special circumstances to protect a recipient who would face total financial ruin if their ex died unexpectedly.

If your ex just refuses to pay, you have to drag them back to court. A judge can order wage garnishment directly from their employer or throw them in jail for contempt of court.

The Tax Implications of Spousal Support

The federal tax rules depend entirely on the exact year your divorce finished.

For divorces finalized after December 31, 2018, the paying spouse uses after-tax dollars. They get zero tax deduction. The person receiving the money gets it tax-free and does not report it as income to the IRS.

If your divorce was finalized before January 1, 2019, you fall under the old rules. The payor deducts the payments on their tax return, and the recipient must claim every dollar as taxable income.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

How long do you have to be married to get alimony?
There is no legal minimum. But marriages under 10 years face a heavy legal presumption against receiving any durational alimony.
Does Florida still have permanent alimony?
No. Senate Bill 1416 eliminated permanent alimony for all new cases filed after July 1, 2023.
Does Florida have a strict spousal support formula?
Florida uses a strict cap, not a guaranteed formula. A judge cannot award more than 35 percent of the difference between the two spouses' net incomes for durational support.
Can alimony be modified?
Yes. Either party can request a modification if they experience a substantial, permanent, and involuntary change in their financial situation.
Is alimony taxable in Florida?
Not for any divorce finalized in 2019 or later. The recipient gets the money completely tax-free.
Does adultery affect alimony?
Yes, but mostly on the financial side. A judge cares if marital money was wasted on the affair.
Can men receive alimony in Florida?
Yes. The law is completely gender-neutral. The court only looks at financial need and the ability to pay.
Can I appeal an alimony decision if I think the judge calculated my net income wrong?
You can file an appeal. You have a very short 30-day window to file the paperwork after the final judgment. You have to prove the judge made a specific legal or mathematical error.
What happens to my alimony if my ex-spouse files for bankruptcy?
Domestic support obligations survive bankruptcy. Your ex cannot wipe out their alimony or child support debt by filing Chapter 7 or Chapter 13.
How do courts calculate income for a spouse who is unemployed on purpose?
The judge looks at their past work history, education, and current local job market. They assign an imputed income based on what that person could realistically earn right now, and base the alimony on that fake number.
